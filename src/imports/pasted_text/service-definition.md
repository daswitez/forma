The Big Idea
This document defines exactly what we sell, who we sell it to, why they buy it, and how to talk about it in a way that makes price irrelevant to the conversation.

We do not sell websites. We do not sell design. We sell the gap closed — between who a business has grown into and how it currently appears to the world.


That framing changes everything. It changes who you target, how you open conversations, what you put in proposals, and how you respond when someone says you are too expensive. Price objections disappear when the client understands they are not buying hours of work — they are buying the version of their business that finally looks as serious as it actually is.

5. How to Sell the Value — Not the Price
This section exists to make every sales conversation more effective. The goal is never to justify the price. The goal is to make the price feel irrelevant compared to the cost of the problem it solves.

The Core Reframe
The question is never: 'Is $7,000 a lot to spend on a website?' The question is: 'What is it costing you every month to show up with a website that undersells your business?'


Price-Focused vs Value-Focused Language
Price-Focused Framing (Avoid)
Value-Focused Framing (Use This)
Our Clarity Sprint costs $2,000.
The Clarity Sprint gives you a complete picture of what's holding your website back — and a clear plan to fix it. Most clients recoup that in a single better-fit deal.
We charge $7,000–$12,000 for a full build.
If your current website is losing you one qualified client a quarter, that's likely costing you more than the entire engagement. We close that gap permanently.
The retainer is $800–$2,000 a month.
For the cost of a few hours of a senior hire, your digital presence keeps improving every month — without you managing it.
Design and development packages starting from...
We start every engagement by understanding your business goals — then build the digital presence that supports them.


The ROI Case — Use This in Proposals
For any business earning over $200,000/year, the maths on this investment is straightforward. Walk clients through it:



If your average client is worth $5,000/year, and your current website is causing you to lose or undersell even two clients a year, that is $10,000 in lost or underpriced revenue annually. A $7,000 engagement that closes that gap pays for itself in year one — and compounds every year after.


You do not need to be aggressive with this framing. Just present it calmly, in the discovery call, after they have told you about a deal they lost or a prospect they were embarrassed to send to the site. Let the maths land.

The Conversation Sequence That Works
01
Open with curiosity, not a pitch
Ask about the business, not the website. Get them talking about where they are now vs where they want to be. The website will come up naturally.


02
Name the gap they have already described
Reflect back what they have said: 'So it sounds like the business has moved forward significantly, but the site still reflects where you were two or three years ago. Is that fair?' They will say yes every time.


03
Link the gap to business cost
Ask: 'Has that ever cost you a deal, or made you hesitant to send someone there?' If they say yes — that is the moment. If they say no — probe gently.


04
Introduce the service as the solution
Only now do you explain what you do. Not as a list of features — as the answer to the problem they have just described. 'What we do is close that gap — we handle strategy, design, and build in one engagement, so you come out with a digital presence that matches where the business is now.'


05
Anchor to outcome, not deliverable
When they ask about price, respond with: 'Before we get to that — what would it mean for the business if this was sorted in the next 90 days?' Get them to articulate the value. Then the price is just the cost of getting there.



6. Discovery Call Framework
The discovery call is not a sales pitch. It is a diagnostic conversation. The goal is to understand their situation so completely that when you present the solution, it feels inevitable — not sold.

Rule: Talk for 20% of the call. Listen for 80%. Your job is to ask questions that help the client articulate the problem in their own words.


#
Question
Why You Ask It
01
How would you describe where the business is today vs 2–3 years ago?
Surfaces the growth gap. Lets them articulate their own evolution — so you can reflect it back.
02
When a prospect looks up your website, what do you hope they think — and what do you think they actually think?
The most powerful question in the deck. Creates emotional honesty about the gap.
03
Have you ever lost a deal — or suspected you might have — where the site played a role?
Revenue-links the problem. Moves it from 'nice to fix' to 'costs us money.'
04
Who are you trying to attract more of right now, and does your site speak to them?
Reveals positioning clarity (or lack of it) and opens the messaging conversation.
05
What has stopped you from addressing this before now?
Surfaces real objections early — budget, time, bad past experiences, prioritisation.
06
If we could wave a wand and your digital presence was perfect in 90 days — what would have changed?
Defines the outcome in their language. Gives you the copy for your proposal.
07
What does success look like 6 months after launch?
Anchors the conversation to business impact. Sets the bar for the engagement.


How to Close the Call
Do not pitch at the end. Summarise and confirm:



"Based on what you've shared, it sounds like [business] has grown significantly — but the website still reflects an earlier version of the company. That gap is showing up in [specific thing they mentioned]. The Clarity Sprint would be the right starting point — it gives you a clear picture of exactly what to change and why, and it sets up the next phase if you want to move further. Would it be useful for me to put together a short proposal based on what we've discussed?"


That close works because it names their problem in their words, proposes a low-risk first step, and asks a question rather than pushing. It does not feel like a close — it feels like a logical next move.

7. Proposal Structure
A proposal should read like a diagnosis followed by a prescription — not a menu of services. It should make the client feel understood before it makes them feel sold to.

Section 1 — Where You Are Now
Summarise the gap in their own words. Show you listened. Reference specific things they said in the discovery call.
Section 2 — What This Is Costing You
Articulate the business impact of the current situation. Revenue, credibility, missed opportunities. Make the problem feel real and costly.
Section 3 — What We Propose
Recommend the specific tier and explain why it fits their situation. Do not list all three tiers — recommend one and explain the logic.
Section 4 — What You Get
List the specific deliverables, timeline, and what you need from them. Be concrete.
Section 5 — What Changes
Paint the picture of the outcome. What will be different in 90 days? How will they feel? What will be possible that is not possible now?
Section 6 — Investment
Present the price last — after they have already felt the value. Frame it as the cost of closing the gap, not the cost of a service.
Section 7 — Next Step
One clear, low-friction next action. Not 'let me know if you have questions.' Something like: 'If this feels right, a signed proposal and 50% deposit gets us started. I can have the onboarding doc to you within 24 hours.'
